Overactive Reuptake
Transporter pumps on the sending neuron (Neuron A) recycle neurotransmitters too aggressively. Dopamine and norepinephrine are pulled back out of the synapse before the signal fully reaches Neuron B. The message starts but never completes.

Lower Baseline Availability
The sending neuron produces and releases less dopamine and norepinephrine to begin with. Even before reuptake clears it, the signal starts weaker. Less supply in the pool means less signal reaching the receiving neuron.

Receptor Insufficiency
Neuron B has fewer receptors or less sensitive receptors for dopamine and norepinephrine. Even when neurotransmitters are present in the synapse, the receiving neuron can't fully pick up the signal. The message arrives but nobody's home to answer.

⚡
How Adderall (Amphetamine) Corrects This
Adderall addresses the dysfunction through a dual mechanism, targeting both the supply and the recycling problem simultaneously. It compensates for receptor insufficiency indirectly by increasing signal volume.
1 Blocks transporter pumps — slows reuptake so neurotransmitters stay in the synapse longer
2 Forces extra release — pushes more dopamine & norepinephrine out of Neuron A into the synapse
3 Compensates for weak receptors — increased volume means even fewer receptors can pick up enough signal
These three mechanisms may be present in any combination and vary by individual.
Medication response helps clinicians infer which mechanisms are most active.
